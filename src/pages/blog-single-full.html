The commonplace book, revived

IC Iris Calloway 6 May 2026 5 min read

Long before the highlighter and the screenshot, careful readers kept a commonplace book: a single notebook into which they copied the lines worth keeping. A sentence from a novel, a stray fact, a recipe, a half-formed argument — all gathered in one place, in one hand, over years. The practice is centuries old, and it may be the perfect antidote to the way we read now.

We consume more words than any generation before us, and remember fewer of them. The commonplace book proposes a different exchange: read less, but keep more.

What goes in

Anything that makes you stop. The test is simple — if a line made you look up from the page, it earns a place in the book. Over time the entries begin to talk to each other, and you find you have been writing a portrait of your own attention without meaning to.

A commonplace book is not a record of what you read. It is a record of what reading did to you. — from the essay

How to begin

  1. Choose one notebook you actually like holding.
  2. Copy by hand — the slowness is the point.
  3. Note the source, but skip the system. Indexes can come later, if ever.

"Read less, but keep more."

Start tonight, with the book already in your hands. The first line you copy will feel small. Give it a few years, and it will feel like a self-portrait.
