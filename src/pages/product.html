When the last keeper of the Aldridge Bay light dies, his daughter Maren returns to a coast she swore she'd left for good. In the lantern room she finds a tin of letters — a hundred years of correspondence the keepers gathered from the tide but never sent on.

As Maren reads, the bay gives up its quiet history: shipwrecks and rescues, feuds and reconciliations, and a single unanswered question that has waited three generations for someone to come home and ask it. The Lantern of Aldridge Bay is a debut of rare warmth — a novel about the stories we keep in the dark, and the small, stubborn lights we leave burning for one another.

Written in luminous, unhurried prose, Eleanor Finch's first novel has already drawn comparisons to the great coastal storytellers. It is a book to be read slowly, by a window, as the weather turns.
